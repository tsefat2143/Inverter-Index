What Color is the Sky in Your World?

Bob Spore — February 6, 2015
Twitter Facebook LinkedIn Flipboard 0

In Part 1 of this series, we discussed how color perception is subjective, based on the quality of light, the viewing environment and also the physical characterisics of the viewer’s eyes.

Color perception is also dependent on whether it is viewed as transmitted light (a computer monitor or television) or reflected light (a printed photograph).

Not all monitors are created equal, either. If you’ve ever been to an electronics store where they might have a wall of televisions, you will notice that while many times the color differences between pictures is small, there is still a difference.

A large cause for color differences and innacuracies is the lack of calibration with the display device. When viewing only one isolated display, the human brain acclimates readily to the inherent color balance of that visual representation, so after a few seconds, the brain accepts and recognizes the image context for what it is and adjusts to an off-color face, for instance. But when displays are compared side by side, the brain has no way to acclimate and you “see” the differences.

If accurate color is what you want, you must calibrate your monitor.

And now we come to another dizzying concept. The differences between the color in the printed piece you’re holding in your hand and that same image as viewed on your computer screen.

RGB VS. CMYK

RGB stands for Red, Green, Blue. The three additive primaries. Although all colors of the visible spectrum can be produced by merging red, green and blue light, monitors are capable of displaying only a limited gamut (range) of the visible spectrum.

In other words, you may be able to see a certain color in real life, but that does not mean your monitor can faithfully represent that color.

CMYK stands for Cyan, Magenta, Yellow and Black. While monitors emit light, inked paper absorbs or reflects specific wavelengths. Cyan, magenta and yellow pigments serve as filters, subtracting various amounts of red, green and blue from white light to produce a selective range gamut of colors. Like monitors, printing inks also produce a color gamut that is only a subset of the visible spectrum, although the range is not the same for both. Consequently, the same art displayed on a computer monitor might not match that same image printed in a publication.

A good way to see the color differences between the CMYK and RGB color spectrums is to look at a color gamut comparison chart. The chart to the right plots the visible color spectrum as the entire circle, and within this is a large triangular area representing the RGB gamut, a somewhat smaller area indicating the Pantonepalette of colors and an even smaller area showing what can be shown in CMYK. You can see that a large portion of the RGB color space is “outside” that of the CMYK space. Again, this shows that some colors can only be approximated, not duplicated.

In other words…

But that’s not to mean that CMYK and printing in general offers an extremely limited palate. Quite the contrary, the available color spectrum is quite vast.

What you see on your monitor or on the paper in your hand might not match the actual, real world color of the depicted item. But, as none of us see the world exactly the same, we have found a way to adjust by having what we can term as acceptable color matches.

So what’s the color of the sky on your world?
